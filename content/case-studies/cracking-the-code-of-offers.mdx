## Overview

Offers on Juspay's payment page were **broken at every layer** — unclear cards, opaque eligibility, scattered discounts. Users couldn't answer one basic question: *"What's the best deal I can get right now?"*

I redesigned the entire offers experience — **entry points, listing, card design, application flow, and payment nudges** — for a system serving **500+ merchants** and **100M+ daily sessions**.

**Problem:** Offers felt like a frustrating maze instead of a conversion lever
**Scope:** Android, iOS, Mobile Web — consumer checkout UX

---

## Problem

The offers experience on HyperCheckout **already existed** — entry points, cards, a listing page. It wasn't built from zero. But UX and UI expectations had moved on, and the existing design hadn't kept up. Merchant feedback was coming in, internal teams were flagging inconsistencies, and when we spoke to real users across different checkout contexts, the gaps were hard to ignore.

Before touching any designs, I wanted to **define the problem properly** — understand what was actually broken, who was feeling it, and why it mattered at Juspay's scale.

Conversations with merchants surfaced configuration pain points. Discussions with internal teams and user-facing PMs revealed patterns in drop-offs and confusion. And looking at the existing UI with fresh eyes made it clear: the offer experience wasn't bad because of one decision — it was **the accumulation of many small gaps** that made the whole feel unreliable.

> What should be a straightforward bonus had become a **trust problem**. Users weren't sure what they were getting, why some offers didn't apply, or which payment method they actually needed.

The friction showed up in a few consistent ways:

- **No clarity on the best deal** — Users couldn't quickly identify which offer gave maximum savings
- **Opaque eligibility** — Users saw "HDFC" and assumed any HDFC card worked — only to discover it was locked to one specific instrument. The gap between *perceived* and *actual* eligibility erodes trust
- **Auto-apply vs. coupon confusion** — Users didn't know the difference, and missed savings because of it
- **Vague messaging** — "Offer Available" told users nothing about the actual discount value
- **Poor offer visibility** — Better deals were buried, reducing the effectiveness of merchant promotions entirely

Two archetypes. Two completely different needs.

**The Deal Hunter** — Will compare every offer before paying. Won't check out until they've found the best deal. Needs: comprehensive visibility, easy comparison, clear eligibility.

**The Straight-to-Pay User** — Already committed. Speed is everything. Will take a deal if it's effortless — but won't hunt for one. Needs: zero friction, auto-applied savings, no decision paralysis.

> The design had to serve both — seamlessly. Optimising for one at the expense of the other was not an option.

---

## Approach

Since HyperCheckout is **highly configurable** per merchant, designs had to be flexible yet structured — consistent enough for users, customisable enough for merchants.

The most critical distinction underpinning the entire UX architecture:

| | Auto-Applied | Coupon-Based |
|---|---|---|
| **User action** | None required | Must manually select & apply |
| **Trigger** | Activates on payment method selection | User enters or picks a code |
| **Visibility** | Always visible | Visible or hidden (SMS/email) |
| **Feedback** | No Apply button — seamless | Needs validation + confirmation |

**Design goals:** Surface offers without disrupting checkout. Make eligibility immediately clear. Let merchants configure what shows — without breaking the user experience.

## Offer Cards

**Pain point:** Users saw an offer card but couldn't tell which payment instrument they needed, or whether the discount actually applied to them.

The offer card is the **atomic unit** of the entire system. Every piece of information has a job:

**1. Offer title first** — Users identify the discount instantly before reading anything else

**2. Payment instrument highlighted** — Exact card type, category, and transaction type required. Removes the ambiguity of "which HDFC card counts"

**3. Description secondary** — Supporting detail for curious users — never the first thing they see

**4. Benefit & eligibility upfront** — For applicable offers, the green amount shows the **exact discount the user gets** — calculated based on that offer's criteria (cart value, payment method, coupon type). No vague "up to X%" — the number reflects their actual saving. For ineligible offers, we show precisely why it doesn't apply — minimum cart value not met, item ineligible, quota exhausted — so the user knows what's blocking them and can act on it

## Offer Listing

**Pain point:** With 20+ offers across multiple payment methods, users had no way to quickly find the best deal — or understand why some offers weren't available to them.

The listing page is structured to **surface the highest value, most actionable offers first:**

- **Manual coupon input at top** — always accessible for users arriving with a code from SMS or email
- **Filter tabs by payment method** — All, UPI, Cards, Wallets, PayLater, Netbanking — critical when merchants have 30+ offers configured
- **Available offers sorted by savings** — Offers linked to saved cards surface first (one tap away), then ranked by max savings
- **Unavailable offers with reasons** — "Add ₹92 to avail", "Ineligible items", "Quota exhausted" — a dead-end becomes a nudge toward conversion

### Inline Offer Details — Payment Method Grid

**Pain point:** We already showed a nudge text above the payment method icon — but it didn't tell the user the actual discount amount or what they'd get. To find out, they had to select the method, go all the way to the UPI app or card CVV screen, and only then see the offer detail. By that point, they couldn't make a conscious, informed decision before committing. The nudge existed, but it wasn't doing its job.

For UPI, wallets, and cards, **offer details now surface inline within the payment grid** — discount amount, eligibility, and a direct apply action — visible before the user taps, so they can decide with full context.

## Payment Nudges

**Pain point:** Users chose payment methods without knowing a specific one had an additional offer — leaving savings on the table.

Offer nudges appear **directly on payment method tiles** in the grid — pill labels, discount callouts, and quick confirmation states — so users see the incentive before they commit to an instrument.

## Impact

New-age merchants like **FirstClub**, **OnePlus**, and **TimePrime** have a high bar for design — their products are polished, their users are discerning, and anything that feels bolted-on gets noticed. Most of them had no offers experience of their own. They handed that responsibility entirely to the payment page.

That raised the stakes. The experience had to feel native — not like a third-party widget dropped into checkout, but like something the merchant built themselves. Theming, typography, motion, hierarchy — everything had to blend.

The result was an offers layer that merchants were proud to ship. Users converted faster, spent less time deliberating, and the experience felt seamless enough that most never knew it wasn't the merchant's own work.

## Reflection

The offers system works as **interconnected surfaces** — entry points, listing, cards, application flow, and payment nudges — all merchant-configurable but governed by consistent design principles.

- **Show, don't hide** — Even ineligible offers are surfaced with reasons. Transparency builds trust and nudges cart modifications
- **Match user intent** — Deal Hunters get comparison tools. Straight-to-Pay users get auto-applied savings with zero friction
- **Reduce cognitive load** — Payment method always visible on cards. Direct "Pay" buttons cut the memorise-then-navigate loop
