## Hey there!

I recently took part in a UX Design hackathon, and the task was to design a checkout experience for an e-commerce platform. I've been feeling underwhelmed by the checkout processes on sites like Amazon and Flipkart — so I decided to let my creative juices flow and cook up something special. A checkout journey that's not just functional but downright delightful.

I was the only solo designer competing against teams of talented designers. And I won.

---

## The Problem

Back in 2024, when I took on this hackathon brief, the e-commerce landscape was crowded with platforms offering diverse products tailored to every kind of user. Each one had its own approach, but as a shopper, I'd noticed online buying had become heavily reliant on trust. I'd dealt with the usual frustrations — receiving the wrong product, poor quality items, and complicated return processes — and it shaped how I approached this problem.

The issues I faced as a user:

- **Complex cart page** with an overload of product information
- **Difficult navigation** between sections — cart, address, payment, review
- **Lack of trust signals** — no reassurance when handing over money
- **Hidden offers section** — discounts buried behind unclear entry points
- **Poorly optimized payment pages** — too many options, no guidance

> Before I start designing, I'm assuming the user has already put some items in their cart.

---

## The Goal

Create a delightful experience that improves conversion rate and lets users complete the checkout process with confidence. No stress, no worries — just pure, hassle-free shopping bliss. Every click feels like a high-five and every step brings us closer to happy customers hitting that "buy" button with confidence.

---

## Cart Page

A calm, focused space where every element earns its place — address with delivery times up top, trust signals built in, offers surfaced upfront, and a transparent bill breakdown.

**Problems I set out to fix:**

- **Wrong address selected by accident** — addresses sit far from where users review items, so mistakes only surface after payment
- **Mixed delivery dates buried inside the list** — users have to scroll to learn when each item actually arrives
- **No safety net against fraud or damaged goods** — once it's delivered, the burden of returns is on the customer
- **Hidden offers and coupons** — discounts live behind unclear entry points, so users pay full price unaware
- **Opaque bill breakdowns** — surprise charges at the final step erode trust right before the tap to pay
- **No way to leave delivery instructions** — gate codes, drop-off notes, and timing live in WhatsApp threads instead of the order

## Cart Item Cards

**Pain points I'm solving:** Most cart cards dump every product detail into one cramped block, hide stock and sale information in tiny grey text, and leave users guessing whether an item will actually ship. People discover "out of stock" only after tapping pay, miss expiring offers because the timer lives elsewhere, and tap the wrong variant because switching means leaving the cart entirely.

One card, four purposeful states — default, limited stock, sale ending, and out of stock — each surfacing the one piece of information that decides the next action.

## Cart Confirm & Review Order

**Pain points I'm solving:** Wrong-address deliveries are one of the biggest sources of failed orders, and most apps bury saved addresses behind two extra taps. Users also lose confidence at the final step because pricing, discounts, and shipping live on different screens — so the total feels arbitrary, and any mistake means restarting the flow.

Two quick checkpoints between the cart and payment — pick the right address, then sanity-check the full order in one place before money moves.

## Guest Checkout

**Pain points I'm solving:** Forced sign-up walls are the single biggest first-time drop-off in checkout. New users abandon when asked to create an account, set a password, and verify an email — all before placing a single order. On the flip side, totally anonymous checkouts cause failed deliveries from typo'd numbers and addresses, which cost the platform money in returns.

A four-step path for new users without an account — phone, OTP, address, review — each step doing exactly one job, with verification light enough to feel frictionless but strong enough to prevent bad data from reaching the warehouse.

## Select Payment Method

**Pain points I'm solving:** Payment is where trust collapses fastest. Returning users have to re-pick the same method every single time. New users face an unranked wall of UPI, cards, wallets, EMI, pay-later, and COD with no idea which one offers the best discount. Hidden charges appear at the last second, and offer-eligible methods don't surface their savings until after selection — so users routinely overpay and lose faith in the platform.

### Quick Checkout

For returning users, picking a payment method again is wasted effort. The last-used method surfaces upfront with the exact amount on a single tappable button — no tabs, no re-entry, no second-guessing.

### Full Payment Page

For users who want a different method, most checkout pages fall apart — endless lists, no offer transparency, and savings hidden until you commit. The full page groups every option clearly with familiar logos, surfaces offer-eligible methods with live savings tags, and updates the total in real time so users always know what they're actually paying.

## Order Tracking

**Pain points I'm solving:** The moment after payment is where confidence wobbles most. Users worry the payment didn't go through, can't tell whether the order is actually moving, and have to dig through emails or call support to find out where their package is. Most apps treat this screen as a static receipt instead of an active reassurance loop.

After payment, users get a clear confirmation, a stage-by-stage live tracker, and a single delivery details view with everything they'd need if something goes wrong — turning the post-payment screen from a dead-end into a trust-building moment.

## Got New Ideas — Code to Get Address

**Pain points I'm solving:** Address entry is one of the most error-prone steps in checkout. Users mistype pincodes, skip landmarks, or paste incomplete addresses — and delivery partners spend hours calling, retyping into their own apps, or marking orders as "address not found". On the customer side, sharing a home address with strangers also feels risky for women, solo renters, and anyone in shared housing.

During the design process, I explored using short, scannable delivery codes as an alternative to typing full addresses. Users would generate a unique code tied to their saved location — delivery partners scan or enter it on arrival, skipping the entire address-typing loop and removing a source of failed deliveries and privacy friction in one move.

## That's a Wrap

This hackathon pushed me to rethink patterns I'd taken for granted. Competing solo against teams made every design decision count — there was no room for filler screens or half-baked flows.

The judges highlighted three things: the trust-building approach to payment UX, the inline editing cart experience, and the overall polish of the end-to-end flow.

> **The lesson:** A great checkout doesn't ask users to trust the platform — it earns trust at every interaction. Every visible price breakdown, every familiar payment logo, every smooth transition whispers: *"You're in good hands."*
